Mercury

The closest planet to the Sun

Overview

Mercury is the smallest planet in our Solar System and the one closest to the Sun. Despite its small size, it is one of the most extreme worlds ever studied. Mercury has almost no atmosphere — only a very thin exosphere made of atoms blasted off its surface by solar radiation and micrometeor impacts. Because it lacks a thick atmosphere to trap heat, temperatures on Mercury swing wildly: during the day, they can reach about 430°C, while at night they can drop to around -180°C. Its surface looks very similar to the Moon, covered with countless impact craters formed over billions of years. One of the largest features is the Caloris Basin, a massive impact crater formed by an ancient asteroid collision. Even though Mercury is small, it is incredibly dense. Its large iron core makes up most of its interior, suggesting it once had a thicker rocky mantle that may have been stripped away by a massive impact early in its formation. Mercury also has a weak magnetic field, which is surprising for such a small planet. This suggests that part of its core is still molten and active. Because of its proximity to the Sun, Mercury is difficult to observe from Earth, which is why space missions like NASA’s MESSENGER have been extremely important in revealing its true nature.

Past & Formation

Mercury formed about 4.5 billion years ago, during the early formation of the Solar System when dust and gas began collapsing under gravity to form the Sun and planets. In its early life, Mercury was likely a much larger planet with a thicker rocky mantle. However, scientists believe that a massive collision with a large asteroid or protoplanet may have stripped away much of its outer layers, leaving behind its unusually large iron core. Over billions of years, Mercury was heavily bombarded by asteroids and comets, especially during the period known as the Late Heavy Bombardment. These impacts created the heavily cratered surface we see today. For most of human history, Mercury was difficult to study because it appears so close to the Sun in the sky. Ancient civilizations, including the Romans, named it after the swift messenger god Mercury, because it moves quickly across the sky compared to other planets. The modern understanding of Mercury began with telescopic observations, but real breakthroughs came much later with space missions: Mariner 10 (1974–1975) was the first spacecraft to fly by Mercury and capture close-up images of its surface. MESSENGER (2011–2015) was the first spacecraft to orbit Mercury, mapping nearly the entire surface and discovering key details about its composition, magnetic field, and polar ice deposits. One of the most surprising discoveries from MESSENGER was that Mercury has water ice inside permanently shadowed craters near its poles, even though it is so close to the Sun. Today, Mercury is considered one of the most scientifically important planets for understanding how rocky planets form and evolve in extreme environments.

NASA’s MESSENGER spacecraft helped reveal Mercury’s composition, magnetic field, and frozen water in shadowed craters.

Interesting Facts

  • One day on Mercury = 176 Earth days
  • One year = 88 Earth days
  • Temperatures: -180°C to 430°C
  • It has ice in shadowed craters
  • No moons or rings
