Earth

Our home in the universe 🌍

Overview

Earth is the third planet from the Sun and the only known planet in the universe that supports life. It is often called the “Blue Planet” because about 71% of its surface is covered with water, mainly oceans. Earth has a unique combination of conditions that make life possible: a stable atmosphere, moderate temperatures, liquid water, and a protective magnetic field. Its atmosphere is mainly made of nitrogen (78%) and oxygen (21%), which supports breathing and protects life from harmful solar radiation. The planet has a dynamic surface made of continents, oceans, mountains, and tectonic plates that constantly move and reshape the land over millions of years. This process, called plate tectonics, is responsible for earthquakes, volcanoes, and the formation of mountains. Earth also has one natural satellite — the Moon — which plays a major role in stabilizing Earth’s rotation and causing ocean tides. From space, Earth appears as a vibrant blue sphere with swirling white clouds, green landmasses, and vast oceans, making it one of the most visually striking planets in the Solar System.

Formation & Evolution

Earth formed about 4.5 billion years ago from dust and gas left over after the formation of the Sun. In its early stage, Earth was extremely hot and constantly bombarded by asteroids and comets. During this early period, a massive collision is believed to have formed the Moon. A Mars-sized object, often called Theia, likely collided with early Earth, ejecting material that eventually formed the Moon. As Earth cooled, a solid crust formed, followed by oceans created from water vapor released by volcanic activity and comet impacts. Over time, the planet developed a stable climate system. The earliest signs of life appeared in the oceans around 3.5 to 4 billion years ago, starting with simple single-celled organisms. Over billions of years, life evolved into complex plants, animals, and eventually humans. Earth’s atmosphere also changed dramatically over time. Early Earth had little oxygen, but photosynthetic organisms like cyanobacteria gradually increased oxygen levels, transforming the atmosphere and enabling complex life to evolve. Human civilization is very recent in Earth’s long history, but it has significantly impacted the planet’s climate, ecosystems, and surface. Today, Earth is studied not just as a planet, but as a complex system where atmosphere, oceans, land, and life are deeply interconnected.
