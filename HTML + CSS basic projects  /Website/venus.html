Venus

Earth’s toxic twin

Overview

Venus is the second planet from the Sun and one of the most extreme environments in the entire Solar System. It is often called Earth’s “sister planet” because it is similar in size, mass, and composition, but its surface conditions are completely hostile. Venus has the thickest atmosphere of any rocky planet. It is made up mostly of carbon dioxide, with clouds of sulfuric acid that completely hide its surface from visible light. Because of this dense atmosphere, Venus experiences a powerful greenhouse effect, trapping heat and making it the hottest planet in the Solar System — even hotter than Mercury, despite being farther from the Sun. Surface temperatures on Venus are around 465°C, hot enough to melt lead. The pressure at the surface is also extremely high — about 92 times Earth’s atmospheric pressure, similar to being nearly 1 kilometer underwater on Earth. The surface of Venus is rocky and volcanic, filled with vast plains, mountains, and thousands of volcanoes. Some evidence suggests that Venus may still have volcanic activity today. Another unusual feature of Venus is its slow and backward rotation. It rotates in the opposite direction to most planets (called retrograde rotation), and one full rotation takes longer than its orbit around the Sun. This means a day on Venus is longer than a year on Venus. Because of its thick clouds, Venus is one of the brightest objects in the night sky, often visible from Earth just after sunset or before sunrise, which is why it is called the “Morning Star” or “Evening Star”.

Formation & History

Venus formed about 4.5 billion years ago, along with the rest of the Solar System, from the same rotating disk of gas and dust that formed the Sun and planets. Early in its history, Venus may have been very similar to Earth. Some scientists believe it could have had liquid water oceans and a more Earth-like climate for a significant period. However, as the Sun slowly became brighter over time, Venus experienced a runaway greenhouse effect. Water evaporated, and because water vapor is itself a greenhouse gas, it trapped even more heat. Eventually, the oceans likely boiled away completely. With no stable water cycle and increasing carbon dioxide in the atmosphere, Venus transformed into the extremely hot, dry, and acidic world we see today. Volcanic activity likely played a major role in reshaping the planet’s surface. Large volcanic eruptions released huge amounts of carbon dioxide, further thickening the atmosphere and intensifying the greenhouse effect. Over time, Venus lost most of its ability to regulate temperature, unlike Earth, which developed carbon cycling through oceans, rocks, and life. Human understanding of Venus has evolved gradually: Ancient civilizations observed Venus as a bright wandering light in the sky. Telescopes revealed cloud cover but no surface details. Space missions like NASA’s Magellan (1990s) used radar to map its surface beneath the clouds. Soviet Venera missions were the first to land on Venus and send back data from its surface, although the extreme heat destroyed most landers within minutes. Today, Venus is one of the most studied planets because it may help scientists understand how Earth-like planets can turn into extreme greenhouse worlds.

Interesting Facts

  • Hottest planet (up to 465°C)
  • Rotates backwards (retrograde rotation)
  • Day is longer than its year
  • Thick toxic atmosphere
  • No moons or rings
